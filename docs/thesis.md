# Thesis

## Core statement

Central Asian civilization did not emerge, endure, and transform through male rulers, armies, scholars, and monuments alone.
It depended on women вЂ” named and unnamed вЂ” as operators of biological, domestic, dynastic, symbolic, linguistic, ritual, and material continuity.

## Strong version of the thesis

This project proposes that women in Central Asia must be studied not as a secondary women topic, but as a foundational analytical category for understanding:
- continuity of households and lineages,
- legitimacy of rule,
- preservation of memory,
- transmission of language and ethics,
- formation of taste, adornment, and luxury demand,
- persistence of sacred and ritual worlds,
- survival of civilizational identity across rupture.

## Named and unnamed women

### Named women
- rulers,
- khatuns,
- queens,
- patrons,
- poetesses,
- women visible in architecture, shrines, and dynastic memory.

### Unnamed women
- mothers,
- daughters,
- craftswomen,
- market participants,
- cooks,
- singers,
- storytellers,
- keepers of household archives,
- transmitters of ritual and language.

## Why this matters

A civilization may celebrate its monuments and scholars, but it survives through continuity work.
Much of that continuity work is gendered, often feminized, and historically under-recorded.
